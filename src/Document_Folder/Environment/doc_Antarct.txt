7 7 years ago Favorite Answer The law of the sea applies whether the ship is carrying scientists, tourists, or cargo. GW doesn't enter into it, and your reference doesn't mention law of the sea at all. According to the Antarctic treaty, the Australian government is responsible for coordinating rescue operations in the area of the Southern Ocean where the rescue took place. Also by treaty, rescue operations at sea are conducted without charge to the rescued party regardless of who does the rescuing and the nearest vessel capable of rendering aid is required to assist. The nearest government search and rescue entity is required to take over the rescue as soon as possible to avoid undue financial or commercial strain on the vessel conducting the initial rescue. Salvage, on the other hand, is subject to commercial contract - one reason that the Russian crew is still on board.
I would have thought that someone who has claimed background as a marine engineer would know this. Those who are calling the crew morons are apparently unaware of the number of ships requiring assistance due to being stuck in ice every year. Ice can be piled up to impassable conditions within hours even in sheltered seas and lakes, nevermind open ocean, and you can't really tell how thick the ice is until you hit it. The U.S icebreaker Glacier, capable of breaking 20 ft ice, was stuck for a while in the Weddell Sea, too remote for rescue, in 1978. Recent headlines have mentioned cargo vessels on the Great Lakes and a 1000 passenger ferry in the Baltic near Stockholm. 0 3 1 Login to reply the answers Post John Lv
